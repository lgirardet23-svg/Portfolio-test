RTeethC: Concentric Tube Dental Robot

Design and development of an innovative opto-mechatronic device for intra-oral monitoring.

Category: CAD / Biomedical Engineering / Mechatronics

1. Final project presentation and branding.

Project Goal üéØ

The RTeethC (Robot √† Tubes Concentriques) was designed to solve a major oral hygiene issue: the difficulty of visually inspecting hidden areas, such as the back of molars. The objective was to create a cost-effective (< 50‚Ç¨), ergonomic, and high-resolution device that allows users to perform a thorough dental self-examination at home.

Technical Solution: Concentric Tube Technology üõ†Ô∏è

Unlike traditional rigid intra-oral cameras, this device uses Concentric Tube Robot (CTR) technology.

  • Active Curvature: Two pre-curved Nitinol (NiTi) tubes are nested within each other. By rotating and translating these tubes relative to one another, the tip of the probe can curve and maneuver around teeth to film "hidden" surfaces.
  • Opto-Mechatronics: The device integrates a high-definition CMOS camera module with LED lighting, powered via a standard USB cable to maintain a lightweight profile.

CAD Design & Manufacturing üíª

The entire mechanical system was modeled to ensure smooth kinematic movement:

  • Mechanical Drive: Designed using a system of gears and DC micro-motors to control the rotation and translation of the Nitinol tubes.
  • Ergonomics: The main casing (ABS plastic) was designed for single-handed use, featuring intuitive dials for real-time control of the probe's curvature.
  • Industrial Feasibility: The project included a full manufacturing study, selecting injection molding for large-scale production of the biocompatible nose and casing parts.

Future Improvements & Perspectives üöÄ

The RTeethC serves as a platform for future smart features:

  • AI Diagnostics: Integration of computer vision to automatically detect plaque or early-stage cavities.
  • Gamification: A dedicated app could provide a "brushing score" to encourage children's dental hygiene.
  • Health Tracking: Archiving inspection history to alert users when a professional dental consultation is necessary.

**Context:** ISIFC 2nd-year Mechanical Design Project (2025-2026). Realized by Lucas Girardet and Lucas Gaudot.

Road Sign Structural Analysis

Finite Element Analysis (FEA) and optimization of a cantilevered motorway sign post.

Category: CAD, Mechanical Analysis (Erasmus Project)

1. Road Sign Drawing.

2. Creo model showing applied loads and anchorage.

3. Creo results showing maximum Von Mises Stress and Deflection.

4. Simulation comparing square vs. circular cross-sections.

5. Simulation comparing hollow vs. solid circular cross-sections.

6. Close-up of stress and table of results at the pole base.

7. Summary table comparing Creo, Ansys, and Mathematical stress results.

Project Goal üéØ

The objective was to design and analyze a lightweight cantilevered steel post structure capable of supporting two motorway road signs. The design had to minimize weight while enduring complex loads, including compressive, torsional, bending, and shear stresses, primarily caused by the signs' weight and a maximum wind speed of 100 km/h (473 N/m¬≤).

Key Stages & Technologies Employed üõ†Ô∏è

  • **FEA Modeling:** Structure modeled in CAD (Creo) and analyzed with Creo Mechanica and Ansys FEA. The final material selected was Structural Steel due to its strength-to-weight ratio and resistance to deflection.
  • **Optimization Study:** Simulations were conducted to optimize the cross-sectional shape for minimal stress and weight. The circular cross-section showed lower Von Mises stress (10.66 MPa) compared to the square cross-section (11.87 MPa) for the same area.
  • **Hollow vs. Solid Design:** Further optimization showed that a hollow circular post (Outer D=200mm, Inner D=180mm) significantly reduced stress and achieved a total weight of 346 kg.
  • **Mathematical Validation:** Stress values were calculated mathematically at four highly stressed points at the base of the pole. The maximum Von Mises stress from the FEA was 73.16 MPa, resulting in a Safety Factor of 2.9.

**Context:** Mechanical Design Lab, Erasmus Semester at **Technological University Dublin**, Ireland (Jan 2024).
